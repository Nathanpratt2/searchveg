If you were eating a plant-based diet prior to the 2020s, you remember the "Dark Ages." There was not as large of a movement or knowledge sharing to enhancing the flavor and convience of plant-based foods. Back then, critics would even declare certain foods "impossible" to make without animal products. But they underestimated the creativity of the food scientists to come like [Addicted to Dates](https://searchveg.com/?q=Addicted+to+Dates), [Rhian's Recipes](https://searchveg.com/?q=Rhian%27s+Recipes), [School Night Vegan](https://searchveg.com/?q=School+Night+Vegan), and many others. 

It's worth noting that many vegans stray away from anything resembling animal products (even if its 100% vegan) but for many born into omnivore-hood, there remains a lurking nostolgia for many things they grew up eating.

Well, as School Night Vegan's book puts it "Anything You Can Cook, I Can Cook Vegan". The impossible has been achieved. Here are 10 things they said couldn't be made vegan—and the ways we pull them off today.

---

## 1. Meringue & French Macarons
**The Challenge:** Egg whites are protein powerhouses that trap air when whipped, creating the stiff peaks required for delicate meringues and finicky macarons. 
**The Magic Trick:** *Aquafaba.* In 2014, a French musician discovered that the leftover brine inside a can of chickpeas mimics the exact protein-to-starch ratio of egg whites. Whip it with a little cream of tartar and sugar, and it fluffs up into gorgeous, glossy white peaks. It completely revolutionized vegan baking.

## 2. Luxury Caviar (Fish Roe)
**The Challenge:** The tiny, delicate "pop" of expensive fish eggs and the briny, oceanic liquid inside them.
**The Magic Trick:** *Spherification.* Borrowed from molecular gastronomy, chefs mix a briny kelp and soy broth with sodium alginate (a natural extract from brown seaweed). They then drop the liquid using a syringe into a calcium bath. The chemical reaction instantly forms a delicate gel membrane around each drop, creating black pearls that pop in your mouth exactly like real caviar.

## 3. Crab Cakes & Lobster Rolls
**The Challenge:** The distinctly flaky, tender, and slightly sweet meat of ocean crustaceans.
**The Magic Trick:** *Hearts of Palm.* The inner core of certain palm trees has a naturally layered, fibrous texture that shreds beautifully. Tossed with Old Bay seasoning, vegan mayo, and a squeeze of lemon, then pan-fried or stuffed into a toasted, buttered roll, it mimics the texture and subtle sweetness of crab and lobster with shocking accuracy.

## 4. Sunny-Side-Up Eggs
**The Challenge:** Replicating a firm egg white surrounding a rich, runny, golden yolk that you can dip toast into.
**The Magic Trick:** *Kala Namak & Agar-Agar.* The "white" is often made from a smooth blend of silken tofu and agar-agar (a seaweed derivative that sets like gelatin). The "yolk" uses pumpkin puree or nutritional yeast for color, thickened with tapioca. The absolute secret weapon is *Kala Namak* (Himalayan black salt), which has a high sulfur content that perfectly mimics the taste and smell of eggs.

## 5. Authentic, Rind-Ripened Brie
**The Challenge:** Replicating the fuzzy, edible white rind and gooey, pungent center of French Brie seemed impossible without dairy milk.
**The Magic Trick:** *Penicillium candidum.* Artisan vegan cheesemakers realized they could use the exact same mold spores used in traditional dairy cheese! By inoculating a rich cashew cream base and aging it in a humidity-controlled cave, the mold blooms and forms that signature white rind, breaking down the cashew proteins into a gooey, funky masterpiece.

## 6. Stretchy, Melty Mozzarella
**The Challenge:** Early vegan cheeses just sat there, sweating under the broiler, refusing to melt. Dairy cheese melts because of casein proteins, which plants don't have.
**The Magic Trick:** *Cultured cashews and tapioca starch.* By blending soaked cashews with traditional cheese cultures (like acidophilus) and adding tapioca starch, the cheese gets an incredible stretch when heated. Today, precision fermentation is even allowing companies to brew real casein proteins using microflora—no cows required.

## 7. Flaky, Buttery Croissants
**The Challenge:** A croissant requires "lamination"—folding cold, solid butter into dough hundreds of times so that the water in the butter steams in the oven, creating flaky air pockets. Standard vegan margarine melts too fast.
**The Magic Trick:** *High-fat vegan butter blocks.* Bloggers and brands finally formulated plant-based butter using blends of coconut, sunflower, and shea oils that mimic the exact melting point of dairy fat (around 90°F / 32°C). The result? Flaky, shatter-crisp pastries.

## 8. Pan-Seared Scallops
**The Challenge:** The buttery, tender, slightly fibrous texture of a sea scallop.
**The Magic Trick:** *King Oyster Mushrooms.* These massive mushrooms have thick, uniform stems. When sliced into rounds, scored in a crosshatch pattern, soaked in a kelp broth, and pan-seared in vegan butter, their texture is shockingly identical to scallops.

## 9. Slow-Smoked Pulled Pork
**The Challenge:** The deeply savory, stringy, fall-apart texture of slow-roasted pork shoulder.
**The Magic Trick:** *Young Green Jackfruit.* Before this massive tropical fruit ripens and gets sweet, its flesh is neutral, starchy, and remarkably fibrous. When shredded, simmered in a savory broth, and baked with BBQ sauce, the fibers absorb all the smoky flavor and break down into tender, meaty strands that easily fool hardcore barbecue fans.

## 10. Fluffy Marshmallows
**The Challenge:** Traditional marshmallows rely entirely on gelatin (which is derived from animal bones and connective tissue) to hold their bouncy, airy shape.
**The Magic Trick:** *Agar-agar.* Derived from red algae, agar-agar has been used in Asian desserts for centuries. When boiled with sugar syrup and whipped furiously with aquafaba or soy protein isolate, it traps the air and sets into perfect, campfire-ready vegan marshmallows.

## 11. Crispy Bacon
**The Challenge:** Bacon’s unique appeal is the contrast between the smoky, salty flavor and the delicate, shattering crispiness of rendered animal fat.
**The Magic Trick:** *Rice paper.* Yes, the same translucent wrappers used for fresh spring rolls! By dipping two sheets of rice paper into a marinade of liquid smoke, soy sauce, smoked paprika, and maple syrup, stacking them together, and baking them, the starches bubble and crisp up into perfect, shatteringly crunchy strips of "bacon" in under 10 minutes.

## 12. Raw Ahi Tuna Poke
**The Challenge:** The translucent, ruby-red color and tender, melt-in-your-mouth texture of raw sashimi-grade tuna.
**The Magic Trick:** *Slow-roasted Watermelon.* It sounds wild, but when you slowly bake or vacuum-compress cubes of watermelon, the water evaporates and the cellular structure drastically condenses, removing the fruit's natural crunch. Toss those dense cubes in soy sauce, toasted sesame oil, ginger, and kelp powder, and the fruit transforms into a savory, tender "tuna" that looks and feels exactly like the real thing.

## 13. Feathery, Pull-Apart Brioche & Challah Bread
**The Challenge:** Enriched doughs get their incredibly soft, shreddable, cake-like crumb from a heavy dose of eggs and dairy butter. The egg yolks provide lecithin (an emulsifier) and fat that coat the flour strands, preventing them from forming tough gluten. 
**The Magic Trick:** *The Tangzhong Method and Aquafaba.* Home bakers borrow an ancient Asian bread-making technique called *Tangzhong*, where a small amount of flour and plant milk is cooked into a thick paste before being added to the dough. This pre-gelatinizes the starches, allowing them to absorb twice as much moisture. Combine that with a little aquafaba (for egg-like binding) and vegan butter, and the resulting bread is impossibly soft, rich, and stays fresh for days.

## 14. Hollow, Puffed Choux Pastry (Éclairs & Cream Puffs)
**The Challenge:** Choux pastry doesn't use yeast or baking powder. Instead, the dough relies entirely on the high water content of eggs. When it hits a hot oven, the water turns to steam, violently puffing the pastry open, while the egg proteins instantly set to hold that hollow, crusty shell so you can fill it with cream. 
**The Magic Trick:** *Soy Milk, Aquafaba, and High Initial Heat.* To recreate this structural miracle, vegan bakers cook a "panade" (a paste of flour, vegan butter, and water) on the stove, then beat in a mixture of high-protein soy milk and whipped aquafaba (or a commercial mung-bean egg replacer). By blasting the pastry at 400°F (200°C) for the first 15 minutes, the plant liquids flash into steam, hollowing out perfect, golden cream puffs.
